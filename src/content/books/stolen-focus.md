Hari spent three years interviewing scientists and experts across twelve causes of the attention crisis. The central argument: we did not lose our focus, it was stolen. Tech platforms are designed to fragment attention because engagement — not your wellbeing — is the product.

The book moves from the personal to the political. Individual fixes like phone detoxes help at the margins, but the real problem requires collective action against the systems built to exploit our cognitive limits.

## Quotes

> Your attention didn't collapse. It was stolen.

> We have built a world that is hostile to focus, and then we blame ourselves for not being able to focus in it.

> Flow is when you are so absorbed in something meaningful that time dissolves. It is one of the best things a human can experience — and we are systematically being stripped of the conditions that produce it.

## Notes

- The chapter on flow states and how distraction culture destroys the conditions for deep work is the strongest part.
- Hari is not anti-technology — he is against the specific business model that monetizes stolen attention.
- The argument for structural solutions (regulation, four-day work weeks) is more convincing than it sounds at first.
